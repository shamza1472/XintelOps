> Through that 21-mile corridor moves approximately 20% of the world's oil supply, and about a third of global LNG. Every day that vessels aren't moving through it is a day the global energy market is pricing in risk.

> Here's the data: as of this week, daily vessel transits have dropped from 138 to 8. That's not a disruption. That's a near-total stoppage driven by insurance abandonment, carrier refusal, and active military threat.

> The economic consequences of a formal Hormuz closure — even a partial one lasting two to three weeks — are severe. Oil prices would spike 15 to 25% immediately. Shipping would reroute around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 14 days to transit times and substantial cost. European energy security, already fragile, would face emergency stress tests. LNG spot markets would detach from long-term contracts. Gulf state economies — even those friendly to the West — would face cascading disruptions to their own export revenues.

> And there is a feedback loop here worth understanding: the more economically disruptive the Strait situation becomes, the more domestic pressure Iranian leadership faces — which historically makes them more likely to escalate, not de-escalate.

> That's one of the most underappreciated risks in this conflict: not deliberate escalation by either major party, but an uncontrolled proxy action that forces a response and collapses whatever diplomatic space remains.

### SCENARIO A — Managed De-escalation (30% probability)

> Both sides agree to a face-saving pause. Iran gets something — sanctions relief, a formal US commitment not to pursue regime change. The US gets something — an Iranian commitment on the Strait and a reduction in proxy activity. A ceasefire holds for 30 to 45 days.

> Why this could happen: there is evidence of a backchannel. A ceasefire proposal was delivered simultaneously to Tehran and Washington. Qatar has been active as a mediator. The fact that the proposal exists at all tells you someone is still talking.

> Why it's harder than it looks: Iran's hardline faction has control. And on the US side, Trump's public framing — the 48-hour ultimatum, the infrastructure destruction threats — makes a private deal politically difficult to execute without being accused of blinking.

### SCENARIO B — Prolonged Elevated Conflict (50% probability)

> Neither side achieves a decisive win. Neither side agrees to a ceasefire. The conflict settles into a sustained high-tempo campaign — strikes and counterstrikes continuing for weeks or months — without crossing into full conventional war.

> This is the most historically common outcome in conflicts of this type. Both sides absorb costs, each believing the other will eventually reach a breaking point first.

> The problem with this scenario is that it's not stable. The longer it runs, the more likely a miscalculation — an Iranian strike that accidentally kills enough US personnel to force a retaliation, a proxy action that hits the wrong target, a nuclear proximity event that triggers an Israeli response that Iran cannot absorb without full escalation.

> Scenario B, in other words, is a path that leads to Scenario C on a delay.

### SCENARIO C — Full Regional Escalation (20% probability)

> A single triggering event — most likely either a strike on a nuclear facility or a mass-casualty event against US or Israeli civilians — collapses the remaining restraint on both sides.

> Israel conducts strikes on Iranian nuclear sites. Iran responds with ballistic missiles at Israeli population centers. The Hormuz Strait is formally closed. Proxy networks fully activate across Yemen, Lebanon, Iraq, and Syria. The US is drawn into direct combat at scale.

> The downstream consequences: oil above $120 per barrel. Supply chain disruptions affecting every import-dependent economy on earth. NATO emergency coordination. China assessing the window for Taiwan. Russia gaining strategic breathing room in Ukraine while Western attention and resources pivot.

> 20% probability is not low. That's 1-in-5. On something with consequences of this magnitude, that's a number that should concentrate attention.
